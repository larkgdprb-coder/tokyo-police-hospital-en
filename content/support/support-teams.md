## Infection control team

### Infection Control Team (ICT)

In order to protect everyone involved in the hospital from infection, ICT works to provide a safe and comfortable hospital life by quickly understanding infection trends throughout the hospital and appropriately managing infection control measures. In addition to our in-hospital activities, we also collaborate with local medical institutions and work to reduce infections throughout the region.

Members: Physician (ICD), Infection Control Certified Nurse, Pharmacist, Bacteriological Laboratory Technician, Administration

#### Main activities

- Ascertaining the status of outbreaks of infectious diseases in the hospital Management of the hospital infection control committee, link nurse association, and ICT meetings Healthcare-related infection surveillance Preparation and revision of the nosocomial infection control manual Understanding and providing guidance on the implementation status of infection control on-site through ICT rounds Staff health management (promotion of vaccine programs, etc.) Staff education Information sharing and coordination of infection control information with other facilities

We will continue to work to implement evidence-based infection prevention measures.

### Antimicrobial Stewardship Team (AST)

AST is a team that supports the proper use of antibiotics in the treatment of infectious diseases in order to provide effective treatment, prevent side effects, and reduce the risk* of the emergence of resistant bacteria.

Members: Comprised of doctors, infection control certified nurses, pharmacists, and bacteriological laboratory technicians, many of whom also belong to ICT and work in collaboration with ICT.

*Inappropriate use or long-term administration of antibiotics can lead to the development or spread of drug-resistant bacteria.

Source: Government Public Relations Online (external link)

#### Main activities

- Monitoring is conducted for cases of use of broad-spectrum antibiotics and patients with positive blood cultures.In the monitoring of infectious disease treatment, AST rounds are conducted once a week (with an infectious disease specialist), and the implementation status of appropriate microbial tests, blood tests, image tests, etc., and the appropriateness of antibiotic selection, usage, and dosage (including TDM) are evaluated over time, and feedback is provided to the attending physician as necessary.Understanding the status of antibiotic use and raising awareness of appropriate use. Monitoring the usage status of notification-based antibiotics (broad-spectrum antibiotics) Creating an antibiogram within the facility (once a year) Creating a pocket manual for antibiotics within the facility (once a year) Education and awareness to promote the proper use of antibiotics Sharing information and collaborating with other facilities on the proper use of antibiotics

We will continue to promote the appropriate use of antibiotics in order to maximize treatment effects for patients, minimize adverse events, and complete the treatment of infectious diseases as quickly as possible.

## palliative care team

Palliative care is care that can be provided from the time a person is diagnosed with cancer until the end of life to relieve physical and mental pain. Therefore, our aim is to alleviate as much as possible the ``suffering'' faced by patients and their families, including not only the painful symptoms caused by the cancer itself, but also the side effects of cancer treatment and the suffering associated with long-term treatment.

Our hospital's palliative care team collaborates with attending physicians in each department to provide palliative care to hospitalized patients. The members are doctors who examine physical and mental health, palliative care certified nurses, pharmacists, medical social workers (MSW), certified psychologists, registered dietitians, and physical therapists. In this way, various professions are involved, and each member utilizes their field of expertise to provide support.

The palliative care team makes rounds every Monday (if Monday is a holiday, special rounds will be held on another day of the week). At that time, we share the patient's treatment policy with the attending physician of each department and ward staff, and examine the patient directly. Based on this, we hold multidisciplinary conferences and assess the patient's situation from multiple perspectives and intervene. In addition, we will visit patients and their families to support them as needed, even outside of the clinic rounds. Furthermore, we are working to spread and raise awareness of palliative care within our hospital by planning in-hospital seminars on palliative care and publishing a publication called ``Palliative Care Newsletter''.

If you are having trouble with any of the following, please contact our palliative care team.


□ If you have physically painful symptoms


Example: I feel nausea, have severe pain, and have difficulty breathing.


□ If you have psychologically distressing symptoms


Example: Feeling anxious or irritated, unable to sleep at night, feeling depressed and not feeling like doing anything


□　About medicines


Example: Anxiety about taking narcotic pain relievers, difficulty taking medicines


□ Concerns about home treatment and hospital transfer (discharge adjustment)


Example: I want to stay at home after being discharged from the hospital, but I don't know how.


There are financial concerns regarding treatment and recuperation.


□　Nutritional guidance, nutritional management


Example: I can’t eat rice, I don’t know what kind of food to eat.


□　About rehabilitation


Example: You are worried about maintaining your physical strength after being discharged from the hospital, you want to know comfortable movements and postures, you want to do something that you can do, etc.

## Nutrition Support Team

This is the Nutrition Support Team (NST) at Tokyo Police Hospital. Our hospital's NST is comprised of all staff involved in the bedside, including doctors, nurses, pharmacists, registered dietitians, clinical laboratory technicians, rehabilitation staff, clinical engineers, and administrative staff.

At NST, we are working to maximize the expertise of each profession to support patients with nutritional management to maintain their physical strength and obtain effective treatment effects. Nutritional support is not just from the perspective of ``nutrition,'' but also an important factor that allows patients to ``enjoy eating and feel satisfied with their meals.'' I want to keep this in mind and work hard every day.

In addition, through activities such as study groups, we work with local medical institutions to improve and enhance nutritional support activities throughout the region.

## respiratory support team

Our team consists of emergency and intensive care physicians, pulmonology physicians, intensive care certified nurses, chronic respiratory care certified nurses, clinical engineers, physical therapists, pharmacists, and administrative staff. The purpose of our activities is to manage the safety of patients who require respiratory therapy and to get them off ventilators as soon as possible. The main activities are: (1) giving advice on ventilator settings and optimizing the treatment environment through in-hospital rounds; (2) responding to consultations on respiratory care; (3) creating and revising manuals; (4) holding in-hospital study sessions; and (5) inspecting and providing guidance on home ventilators brought to the hospital.

## Early mobilization/rehabilitation team

In recent years, the importance of rehabilitation in the field of intensive care has been recognized, and in the 2018 revision of medical fees, a new evaluation was established for early mobilization and rehabilitation efforts by multiple professions. By holding a team conference within 48 hours of admission to an intensive care center and performing early bed transfer and rehabilitation interventions, it is effective to prevent acute-stage patients from deteriorating their ADL (activities of daily living) and improve their respiratory status. It is also known that active rehabilitation increases patients' acceptance of treatment and motivation for improvement, and is expected to improve prognosis. Since May 2018, our center has formed a team of 2 intensive care certified nurses, 4 emergency and intensive care doctors with over 5 years of intensive care experience, and 4 physical therapists, and has been holding conferences every morning from Monday to Saturday, even on holidays when people are available. Severe cases require early rehabilitation intervention, and we perform multidisciplinary team rehabilitation, including clinical engineers to safely respond to patients on life support devices such as ventilators, pharmacists to adjust anesthesia appropriate for rehabilitation, and registered dietitians to ensure adequate nutritional management.

## Bedsore countermeasure team

Bedsores are also commonly referred to as "bedsores." Wounds are caused by continuous pressure on areas that come into contact with bedding, beds, wheelchairs, etc., which impairs blood flow to the skin, damaging the skin and underlying tissues. It is said to be more likely to occur in people who cannot eat enough, are unable to move their bodies on their own, or are unable to defecate in the toilet. It is important to prevent pressure ulcers from occurring because it takes time to heal and can affect your daily life. Our hospital's pressure ulcer control team is comprised of plastic surgeons, nurses, pharmacists, registered dietitians, physical therapists, and administrative staff. The pressure ulcer control team holds conferences and rounds for patients at high risk of developing pressure ulcers and patients with pressure ulcers.Each position utilizes its expertise and works in collaboration with pressure ulcer link nurses and NSTs (Nutritional Support Teams) in the wards, outpatient clinics, and operating rooms, with the aim of prevention and early healing of pressure ulcers.

## Diabetes dialysis prevention team

In order to suppress the progression of chronic kidney disease, it is important to continue treatment with drugs, but it is also important to pay attention to your diet and lifestyle habits. This will prevent not only kidney disease but also other complications. In order to slow the progression of kidney disease and ultimately prevent kidney failure, we offer a program that includes not only regular outpatient examinations, but also nutritional guidance by registered dietitians and lifestyle treatment guidance by nurses. The program for outpatients with kidney damage due to diabetes (diabetic kidney disease) has been in place since October 2013, but from June 2024 it will also be implemented for outpatients with chronic kidney disease without diabetes. There are four full-time physicians in the Department of Nephrology and Metabolism. In addition, five nurses (as of April 2025) who belong to the Blood Purification Center and are experts in the treatment and nursing of kidney disease patients will take turns giving advice on daily life. In addition, three registered dietitians (as of April 2025) take turns to provide detailed nutritional guidance. The process is that after nutritional guidance, a nurse will provide guidance on lifestyle treatment, and finally a doctor's examination. Although there is no set number of sessions, we will conduct the session at least 3 times and, depending on the patient's request, we will discuss whether to continue the session or not. There are also patients who have previously undergone the program but have had a blank period, but who decide to undergo the program again after seeing the progress of their kidney disease.

## Urinary care team

``No matter how old I get, I want to go to the toilet and defecate on my own until the end.'' Isn't this a wish that everyone has? In the FY2016 revision of medical fees, it was recognized that it is important for hospitalized patients to urinate independently in their lives after discharge, and an item regarding urinary independence was added for the purpose of providing management and guidance regarding urination. The purpose of urinary independence instruction is to ``remove the urinary catheter as soon as possible, prevent urinary tract infections, and enable patients to manage their urination on their own.'' It is expected that urinary independence guidance will protect the patient's dignity as a person, maintain and improve activities of daily living (ADL), and lead to early discharge from hospital and a reduction in the number of bedridden patients. Therefore, the urinary care team plays a central role in promoting independence in urinary urination. At our hospital, we have formed a urinary care team consisting of a urologist, two nurses, and two physical therapists, and have started our activities in February 2021. For example, one symptom of lower urinary tract dysfunction is urinary retention. For patients with urinary retention, the urinary care team discusses the patient's cognitive function, excretion status, and where the problem lies in the lower urinary tract, and then doctors, nurses, and physical therapists provide comprehensive care. Ward nurses play a central role in providing comprehensive care to patients. Specifically, urinary care refers to teaching self-catheterization, administering oral medications prescribed by a doctor, and providing rehabilitation interventions to help patients become independent in the series of movements related to excretion. In addition, urinary care includes selecting clothing and arranging an environment that makes it easy for patients with motor dysfunction to defecate, teaching patients with stress urinary incontinence to strengthen their pelvic floor muscles, and guiding patients to the toilet after a set period of time for patients who are incontinent because they are unable to maintain a urinary rhythm. In order to expand our intervention to patients with lower urinary tract dysfunction outside of the urology department, we have assigned urinary care link nurses to each department and hold link meetings once a month to work to improve the quality of nursing care for lower urinary tract dysfunction in each department. By reducing unnecessary urinary catheter placement, it is expected to reduce the number of bedridden patients. Also, by going to the toilet and defecating, you can prevent a decline in physical strength and muscle strength. Our urinary care team will support you so that you can remain independent in urinating, which is a physiological human need.

## rapid response team

Our hospital's RRS (Rapid Response System) began operation in April 2023 after a period of preparation. RRS is a system that aims to improve the prognosis by noticing sudden changes in patients early on before unexpected cardiac arrest and intervening as a team. Our hospital's response team is comprised of emergency center physicians, emergency center nurses, including intensive care certification, and in-hospital lifesaving technicians. It is said that killer symptoms (dangerous signs linked to sudden changes) often appear before a sudden change, and while keeping this in mind, we create RRS activation criteria: respiratory rate, SpO2, systolic blood pressure, heart rate, and state of consciousness based on the National Early Warning Score (NEWS), which is a score for predicting the prognosis of acute diseases.Furthermore, if there are any concerns about the patient, the nurses in the ward will contact the RRS team at their discretion. After RRS examines the patient, the team's doctors will coordinate treatment with the medical department and, if necessary, admit the patient to the intensive care unit. It is known that RRS, which responds before sudden changes occur, leads to improved patient prognosis, and is being introduced at each hospital. Our hospital is currently only operational from 8:15 to 17:00, but we are aiming to have staff available 24 hours a day, including holidays.

## Dementia delirium care support team

At our hospital, we have established a Dementia Delirium Care Support Team to support people with dementia and those with declining cognitive function so that they can live their lives in their own way. The members are psychiatrists, dementia nurses who have completed the certified nurse education program, ward nurses, pharmacists, occupational therapists, and administrative staff. When a person with dementia is hospitalized, due to poor physical condition due to the disease and treatment in an unfamiliar environment, the patient may not be able to do the things they were used to doing well, and may become anxious and confused. It is necessary to understand the physical and mental pain of such patients and provide support so that they can receive treatment safely and with peace of mind. We hold regular in-hospital rounds and team conferences, obtain information from nurses assigned to each department, and consider support from multiple perspectives. By doing so, we will be able to engage in cross-disciplinary activities, consider interventions tailored to each individual, and strive to provide care.

## Physical restraint optimization team

Physical restraint is an act that restricts human behavior and is a serious issue that affects human dignity. Our hospital has ``Guidelines for Appropriate Physical Restraints,'' and our philosophy is not to use physical restraints as a general rule. The physical restraint optimization team consists of the vice director, a psychiatrist, a nurse who has completed the dementia nursing certified nurse education course, a ward nurse, a social worker, an occupational therapist, a pharmacist, and administrative staff. In acute care, physical restraints may be unavoidable for treatment purposes in order to protect the safety and well-being of patients. In such cases, the Physical Restraint Appropriation Team, which is made up of staff from various professions, works together to maintain the dignity and safety of patients and to avoid using physical restraints as much as possible.
