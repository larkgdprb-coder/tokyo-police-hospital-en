## Informed Consent at Tokyo Police Hospital

Informed consent is a cornerstone of ethical medical practice and patient-centered care. At Tokyo Police Hospital, we are committed to ensuring that every patient has the information they need to make meaningful, informed decisions about their medical care.

## What Is Informed Consent?

Informed consent is the process by which a healthcare provider:
1. Explains a proposed treatment, procedure, or test to the patient
2. Discusses the expected benefits and potential risks
3. Presents alternative options
4. Answers the patient's questions
5. Obtains the patient's voluntary agreement before proceeding

Consent must be given **freely and without coercion**, and the patient must have sufficient **mental capacity** to understand the information provided.

## Comprehensive (Blanket) Consent

Upon admission or initiation of treatment, patients are asked to sign a **Comprehensive Consent Form** (*Hōkatsuteki Dōi*). This form covers:

- Routine examinations and standard nursing procedures
- Use of medical information for educational and quality improvement purposes (with anonymization)
- General communication with family members or authorized representatives
- Emergency treatment procedures when the patient is unable to give consent in the moment

This comprehensive consent **does not** replace specific informed consent for:
- Surgical procedures
- Invasive diagnostic procedures
- Experimental treatments or clinical trials
- Blood transfusions (see [Blood Transfusion Policy]({{< relref "blood-transfusion" >}}))
- Any procedure with significant risk

## Specific Informed Consent

Before any surgical or invasive procedure, your physician will schedule a separate consultation to:
- Explain the procedure in detail using diagrams, models, or written materials
- Discuss risks, benefits, and alternatives
- Allow time for you to ask questions
- Obtain your written consent on a procedure-specific consent form

You have the right to take as much time as you need before signing. Please do not hesitate to ask for a second explanation or to consult a family member.

## Consent for Minors and Incapacitated Patients

- For **patients under 15 years of age**, consent is obtained from a parent or legal guardian.
- For **patients who lack decision-making capacity**, a legally authorized representative (family member, legal guardian) provides consent.
- In **life-threatening emergencies**, treatment may be initiated without prior consent if the patient or representative cannot be contacted in time.

## Right to Withdraw Consent

Patients may withdraw consent at any time, even after a procedure has begun (where clinically possible). If you wish to withdraw consent for any reason, please inform your physician or nurse immediately.

## Questions

If you have any questions about the consent process, please speak with your attending physician or contact the Patient Services desk at the main reception.
